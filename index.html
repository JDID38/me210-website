Autonomous Curling Robot

ME210: Introduction to Mechatronics — Stanford University

Project Overview

Our team designed and built an autonomous curling robot for the ME210 final competition. We initially brainstormed the simplest possible approach: a robot that would just drive forward and push the puck toward the target. But early kinematic models showed that controlling a pushing trajectory would be too imprecise, so we pivoted to a stationary flywheel launcher design early in the process and never looked back.

The final robot uses a mecanum-wheel drivetrain on a laser-cut acrylic chassis (rebuilt after our first Duron frame proved too weak), a dual-flywheel launcher with 3D-printed motor couplers, and a servo-driven puck stacker. An Arduino Mega runs a fully non-blocking state machine that follows a line across the table, strafes into position, and executes a timed launch sequence, all without a single call to delay().

We were the very last team to get checked off before the competition started, but that stressful push paid off: in our first round the robot ran its state machine flawlessly, followed the line, and placed the puck right on the target while our opponent's robot got stuck on the table. This portfolio documents the mechanical, electrical, and software journey that got us there.

The Chassis Story

The mechanical architecture of our curling robot was built entirely around our laser-cut acrylic chassis. We chose acrylic after our initial Duron prototype proved too flexible, as we needed a completely rigid foundation that would not vibrate when the high-speed flywheels were spinning. We designed this main base plate in our computer-aided design software, carefully mapping out pre-cut mounting holes for every single hardware component before we actually manufactured it.

To keep the physical assembly organized and functional, we built the robot using a unique angled structure. Instead of making a second horizontal platform for our components, we designed an extension to the puck slide. This extension was built with a slight angle, guiding the pucks smoothly into the firing mechanism while still allowing the high-speed flywheels full contact to grip and launch them. We bolted the heavy DC drive motors and the main flywheel assembly directly to the bottom plate to keep our center of gravity low, which improved our driving stability. The electronics, including our Arduino Mega and motor drivers, were securely mounted to keep the wiring safe from the spinning wheels and the puck loading area.

Connecting the custom launcher parts to this rigid chassis required a lot of care. We used thick machine screws to tightly lock our 3D-printed motor mounts to the front of the acrylic base plate. The servo and the puck stacker wheel were also bolted directly near the central section to feed the angled slide. The line-following sensor array was attached to a small bracket that extended just off the front edge of the bottom plate, placing the sensors at the exact height needed to read the track tape accurately without scraping the floor.

Why Mecanum Wheels

The curling field requires the robot to find specific tape lines and align perfectly with a distant target. Standard wheels would require the robot to drive forward and then physically pivot to correct its path. Pivoting changes the angle of the robot, which would instantly ruin the aim of our stationary launcher. Mecanum wheels completely solve this problem by allowing holonomic movement. This special design lets the robot strafe directly left or right to center itself on a line without ever changing its forward-facing angle.

Even though we had to adapt our chassis mounting brackets at the last minute to fit the borrowed lab wheels, this actually worked. Being able to slide smoothly sideways into the starting box or perfectly center the robot over the launch line without rotating allowed our code to remain simple and our mechanical aim to stay perfectly straight.

Electrical Design

Because we spent most of our timeline on mechanical redesigns, electrical integration happened fast and exposed power limits we had not anticipated. Below is the KiCad schematic for the full system, followed by what we learned about each subsystem.

Power Distribution

A 12 V LiPo feeds two separate paths: a buck converter drops to 5 V for the Arduino and sensor logic, while the motors draw directly from 12 V through high-current MOSFETs. Schottky diodes block back-EMF from the flywheels.

Spinning up the heavy flywheels pulled a massive stall current that we did not anticipate during design. During our first code tests we actually burned through several motor driver ICs, forcing us to add inline fuses and switch to beefier power electronics before we could run the launcher reliably.

Arduino Mega Wiring

The Mega drives four mecanum motors via dual-H-bridge modules (direction on D22-29, PWM on D2-5), a flywheel launcher (BTS7960 enables D30-31, PWM D6), a servo (D8), and five analog line sensors (A0, A12-A15).

We wrote small, separate test scripts for each subsystem first (flywheels, servo dropper, drive motors) to understand real-world behavior before combining everything into one program. This modular approach was critical because our coding phase started very late after weeks of mechanical rebuilds.

High-Current Motor Drivers

Our initial L298N drivers failed catastrophically when flywheel inrush currents exceeded 20 A per motor. We replaced them with BTS7960 H-bridge modules rated for 43 A continuous, which include built-in thermal shutdown and current sensing.

Even with the BTS7960s, raw spin-up was too aggressive. We added a firmware soft-start ramp (PWM increases over 500 ms) to limit inrush and protect both the drivers and the motor brushes. This was one of the first things we coded once the hardware was physically assembled.

void setup() {
  Serial.begin(9600);
  setUpPins();
  state = INIT;
}

void loop() {
  updateServoMotion();
  checkGlobalEvents();

  switch (state) {
    case INIT:   strafeRight();  break;
    case FWD:    moveForward();  break;
    case LEFT:   strafeLeft();   break;
    case RIGHT:  strafeRight();  break;

    case LAUNCH:
      stopMoving();
      spinFlywheels();

      if (!servoSweepStarted &&
          (millis() - launchStartTime >= FLYWHEEL_LEAD_MS)) {
        setServoTarget(SERVO_END_ANGLE);
        servoSweepStarted = true;
      }

      if (servoSweepStarted && !servoReachedEnd &&
          isServoAtTarget() &&
          servoTargetAngle == SERVO_END_ANGLE) {
        servoReachedEnd     = true;
        servoReachedEndTime = millis();
      }

      if (servoReachedEnd &&
          (millis() - servoReachedEndTime >= FLYWHEEL_LAG_MS)) {
        resetServoToStart();
        state = STOP;
      }
      break;

    case STOP:
      stopEverything();
      break;
  }
}

/*--------------- Servo Helpers ----------------------------*/

void setServoTarget(int targetAngle) {
  if (targetAngle < 0)   targetAngle = 0;
  if (targetAngle > 180) targetAngle = 180;
  servoTargetAngle = targetAngle;
}

void resetServoToStart(void) { setServoTarget(SERVO_START_ANGLE); }

void updateServoMotion(void) {
  unsigned long now = millis();
  if (now - lastServoStepMs < SERVO_STEP_INTERVAL_MS) return;
  lastServoStepMs = now;
  if (state != LAUNCH) return;

  if (servoCurrentAngle < servoTargetAngle) {
    servoCurrentAngle += SERVO_STEP_SIZE;
    if (servoCurrentAngle > servoTargetAngle)
      servoCurrentAngle = servoTargetAngle;
    launchServo.write(servoCurrentAngle);
  } else if (servoCurrentAngle > servoTargetAngle) {
    servoCurrentAngle -= SERVO_STEP_SIZE;
    if (servoCurrentAngle < servoTargetAngle)
      servoCurrentAngle = servoTargetAngle;
    launchServo.write(servoCurrentAngle);
  }
}

Software

Coding was pushed to the very end after weeks of mechanical rebuilds, so efficiency was critical. We built a fully non-blocking state machine, eliminated every delay(), and spent hours hand-tuning motor speeds and sensor thresholds on the competition table.

State Machine Architecture

State Machine & Non-Blocking Launch Sequence

Basic linear programming was not enough. The robot needed to read line sensors constantly while also ramping flywheels over several seconds. We completely removed delay() and built a state machine where every timed action uses millis() comparisons. The LAUNCH state sequences flywheel spin-up, a 500 ms lead, a servo sweep, and a 1-second lag, all without blocking the main loop.

me210_robot.ino — state machine core 
enum State_t {
  INIT,    // Strafe right until center line detected
  FWD,     // Drive forward along the line
  LEFT,    // Correct leftward drift
  RIGHT,   // Correct rightward drift
  LAUNCH,  // Flywheel spin-up → servo sweep → stop
  STOP     // All motors off (terminal)
};

State_t state = INIT;

// Non-blocking servo motion state
unsigned long lastServoStepMs     = 0;
int           servoCurrentAngle   = SERVO_START_ANGLE;
int           servoTargetAngle    = SERVO_START_ANGLE;

// Launch phase timing (all millis()-based, never blocks)
unsigned long launchStartTime     = 0;
unsigned long servoReachedEndTime = 0;
bool          servoSweepStarted   = false;
bool          servoReachedEnd     = false;

void loop() {
  updateServoMotion();   // non-blocking incremental servo step
  checkGlobalEvents();   // line-sensor edge detection → state transitions

  switch (state) {
    case INIT:   strafeRight();  break;
    case FWD:    moveForward();  break;
    case LEFT:   strafeLeft();   break;
    case RIGHT:  strafeRight();  break;

    case LAUNCH:
      stopMoving();
      spinFlywheels();

      // Phase 1: after FLYWHEEL_LEAD_MS (500 ms), begin servo sweep
      if (!servoSweepStarted &&
          (millis() - launchStartTime >= FLYWHEEL_LEAD_MS)) {
        setServoTarget(SERVO_END_ANGLE);
        servoSweepStarted = true;
      }

      // Phase 2: detect when servo reaches its end angle
      if (servoSweepStarted && !servoReachedEnd &&
          isServoAtTarget() &&
          servoTargetAngle == SERVO_END_ANGLE) {
        servoReachedEnd     = true;
        servoReachedEndTime = millis();
      }

      // Phase 3: hold flywheels for FLYWHEEL_LAG_MS (1 s), then stop
      if (servoReachedEnd &&
          (millis() - servoReachedEndTime >= FLYWHEEL_LAG_MS)) {
        resetServoToStart();
        state = STOP;
      }
      break;

    case STOP:
      stopEverything();
      break;
  }
}

// Incremental servo stepper — called every loop(), never blocks
void updateServoMotion() {
  if (millis() - lastServoStepMs < SERVO_STEP_INTERVAL_MS) return;
  lastServoStepMs = millis();
  if (state != LAUNCH) return;

  if (servoCurrentAngle < servoTargetAngle) {
    servoCurrentAngle += SERVO_STEP_SIZE;
    if (servoCurrentAngle > servoTargetAngle)
      servoCurrentAngle = servoTargetAngle;
    launchServo.write(servoCurrentAngle);
  } else if (servoCurrentAngle > servoTargetAngle) {
    servoCurrentAngle -= SERVO_STEP_SIZE;
    if (servoCurrentAngle < servoTargetAngle)
      servoCurrentAngle = servoTargetAngle;
    launchServo.write(servoCurrentAngle);
  }
}

// Edge-detected line sensors drive state transitions
void checkGlobalEvents() {
  if (state == LAUNCH) return;   // freeze line-following during launch
  if (testForCenterBlack())      respToCenterBlack();
  if (testForLeftBlack())        respToLeftBlack();
  if (testForRightBlack())       respToRightBlack();
  if (testForRightEdgeBlack())   respToRightEdgeBlack();
  // ... symmetric white-edge handlers omitted for brevity
}

void respToCenterBlack() {
  if (state == INIT || state == LEFT || state == RIGHT) state = FWD;
}
void respToLeftBlack()  { if (state == FWD) state = LEFT;  }
void respToRightBlack() { if (state == FWD) state = RIGHT; }
void respToRightEdgeBlack() {
  if (state == FWD) enterLaunchState();
}

void enterLaunchState() {
  launchStartTime   = millis();
  servoSweepStarted = false;
  servoReachedEnd   = false;
  stopFlywheels();
  state = LAUNCH;
}

Mecanum Drive & Hardware Helpers

Small differences between the cheap DC motors and the borrowed lab wheels caused the robot to crab-walk sideways. We spent a lot of time manually tuning the base PWM speed for each individual wheel (65, 70, 65, 75) until the robot drove straight. Strafing uses a 2× multiplier for adequate lateral force. The sensor latch pattern fires once per black/white transition, and we had to calibrate thresholds to the actual room lighting to eliminate phantom detections from ambient shadows.

  applyWheelSpeeds(2);   // 2x speed for lateral motion
  digitalWrite(FL_IN1, HIGH); digitalWrite(FL_IN2, LOW);
  digitalWrite(FR_IN1, LOW);  digitalWrite(FR_IN2, HIGH);
  digitalWrite(RL_IN1, LOW);  digitalWrite(RL_IN2, HIGH);
  digitalWrite(RR_IN1, HIGH); digitalWrite(RR_IN2, LOW);
}

void strafeLeft() {
  applyWheelSpeeds(2);
  digitalWrite(FL_IN1, LOW);  digitalWrite(FL_IN2, HIGH);
  digitalWrite(FR_IN1, HIGH); digitalWrite(FR_IN2, LOW);
  digitalWrite(RL_IN1, HIGH); digitalWrite(RL_IN2, LOW);
  digitalWrite(RR_IN1, LOW);  digitalWrite(RR_IN2, HIGH);
}

void stopMoving() {
  digitalWrite(FL_IN1, LOW); digitalWrite(FL_IN2, LOW);
  digitalWrite(FR_IN1, LOW); digitalWrite(FR_IN2, LOW);
  digitalWrite(RL_IN1, LOW); digitalWrite(RL_IN2, LOW);
  digitalWrite(RR_IN1, LOW); digitalWrite(RR_IN2, LOW);
  analogWrite(FL_EN, 0); analogWrite(FR_EN, 0);
  analogWrite(RL_EN, 0); analogWrite(RR_EN, 0);
}

// Flywheel control for the puck launcher
void spinFlywheels() {
  digitalWrite(FLY_L_EN, HIGH);
  digitalWrite(FLY_R_EN, HIGH);
  analogWrite(FLY_LPWM, FLY_TARGET_PWM);  // ~59% duty
}

void stopFlywheels() {
  digitalWrite(FLY_L_EN, LOW);
  digitalWrite(FLY_R_EN, LOW);
  analogWrite(FLY_LPWM, 0);
}

// Edge-latched sensor reads (fires once per black→white transition)
bool isLeftOnBlack = false;

uint8_t testForLeftBlack() {
  if (analogRead(LINE_L) > LINE_L_MIN && !isLeftOnBlack) {
    isLeftOnBlack = true;
    return 1;
  }
  return 0;
}

uint8_t testForLeftWhite() {
  if (analogRead(LINE_L) <= LINE_L_MIN && isLeftOnBlack) {
    isLeftOnBlack = false;
    return 1;
  }
  return 0;
}

Reflection

Looking back at the development of our autonomous curling robot for ME210, our team focused heavily on mechanical design long before we wrote any code. Working with Caleb and Daniel, our first idea during the brainstorming phase was to build the simplest robot possible. We wanted to design a robot with no flywheels that would just drive forward and physically push the puck toward the target. Another team actually did this successfully. However, when we looked at the kinematics and the required precision in our early models, we realized this pushing method would be too difficult to control. Because of this, we changed our plan to a stationary launcher design very early in the process.

We spent the first few weeks heavily focused on computer-aided design and physical prototyping, which quickly revealed hidden structural flaws. During our early testing, we discovered that our initial choice of material for the chassis was not strong enough. We originally laser-cut the main frame out of Duron, but it lacked the robustness needed to support the heavy motors and withstand the vibrations of the launcher. Because of this structural weakness, we had to completely recut and assemble the chassis using acrylic to ensure a rigid and durable foundation.

Once we had a stronger acrylic frame, our hardware integration faced an immediate logistical setback. The mecanum wheels we ordered were not delivered in time for the assembly. To keep the project moving, we had to borrow standard mecanum wheels from the university laboratory. These wheels were not in our original design, and they had a very different size and friction coefficient than what we had modeled. This forced us to rapidly adapt our new acrylic chassis and its mounting points to fit the unexpected parts.

While adapting the frame to the borrowed wheels, we found another big mechanical issue with the launcher itself. Connecting our high-speed DC motors to the curling flywheels was much harder than we expected. Standard hardware parts could not attach the motor shafts to our custom wheel hubs without creating a terrible wobble that absorbed all the kinetic energy. To fix this, we used 3D printing to design and test custom motor shafts and couplers to get the precise fit we needed. We also fixed a major issue with the puck loading mechanism during this mechanical phase. The servo kept slipping against the puck stacker wheel because friction was not strong enough. We solved this by adding a specific screw hole to pin the servo horn directly to the stacker wheel, creating a solid physical connection.

Because we spent so much of our timeline perfecting these physical mechanisms and rebuilding the chassis out of acrylic, the coding part was pushed to the very end of the process. We knew we had to be highly efficient with our remaining time, so we took a modular approach. Instead of trying to write the whole program at once, we wrote small, separate test scripts to understand how the high-current flywheels and the servo puck dropper worked in reality before combining them.

When we finally started running our code on the fully built robot, we immediately hit a physical power limit. Spinning up the heavy flywheels pulled a massive initial stall current. During these first code tests, we actually burned a few motor drivers because they could not handle the power spikes. Because of this electrical failure, we had to write smooth software ramp-ups in our code and use better power electronics to handle the high current safely.

As we combined these separate test scripts into one main program, we saw that basic linear programming was not enough. We had to build a non-blocking state machine to manage what the robot was doing. We completely removed delay functions from our code and only used timer evaluations. This allowed the robot to read its line sensors constantly while also spinning up the motors over several seconds. However, when we tested the driving logic, we found that small differences in the cheap DC motors and the borrowed lab wheels made the robot lean and drift to the side. We had to spend a lot of time manually tuning the base motor speeds in our code to fix this crab-walk effect and make the robot drive straight.

After we fixed the driving code, we moved to the competition table to test the whole system, but our line sensors started failing. One of the worst issues was getting phantom line detections. We learned how important it is to calibrate the analog sensor thresholds based on the actual lighting in the room to filter out ambient shadows from the real black tape. Without this calibration, the robot would just strafe off the track or stop its motors for no reason.

Fixing the sensor thresholds helped the robot follow the line, but it revealed a final logic flaw in our code. Driving out of the starting box and the launch zone was very tricky because those areas are surrounded by the exact same black tape the robot is looking for. We solved this issue by adding arming delays to the code. We programmed the robot to intentionally ignore the edge sensors for a short time when leaving a zone, giving the physical chassis enough time to drive past the tape boundaries.

Solving these final software bugs was a massive race against time, especially since we started the coding phase so late. All of these hardware adaptations, material changes, and software problems added up and pushed our schedule to the absolute limit. In fact, our team was the very last one to get checked off right before the competition started. However, this stressful final push proved that our design was robust. In the very first round of the competition, our hard work paid off. The opponent robot had a software bug and got stuck on the table. Meanwhile, our robot ran its state machine perfectly, followed the line, and placed the puck right on the target.

Winning that first round after checking off last felt amazing for the whole team. This project really showed us that mechatronics requires a solid physical foundation before any programming can happen. Moving from CAD models to a moving robot on the competition table forced us to constantly fix the gap between our early mechanical designs, electrical limits, and late-stage software logic.
